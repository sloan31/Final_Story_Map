Background

Interstate 90 (I-90) was widened and renumbered in the 1950-60’s to meet the American Interstate Highway System criteria, thus creating Interstate 90. This remodeling kept traffic moving for the next 30 years.

In 2005, the gas tax provided $551 million to widen I-90, build new infrastructure to create wildlife crossing structures, stabilize rock slopes, prevent avalanches, and expand chain-up and chain-off areas. The project aims to improve 15 miles of I-90 from Hyak to Easton. Construction began in 2009, completing phase one in 2018 and phase two was completed in 2019. Phase one and two improvements included replacement of bridges, widened lanes, the first wildlife overcrossing in Washington, multiple wildlife underpasses, and stabilization of rock slopes.

In 2015, Legislature secured $426 million with the Connecting Washington funding package to finish the construction of the final eight miles of the corridor.

Road Kill Data

The I-90 division of the north and south cascade mountain range limits wildlife movement and proved to be dangerous to humans as well. I-90’s high volume of traffic and location lead to numerous car accidents prior to 2019. With the completion of multiple wildlife crossings and fencing placed along the highway, there has been a decrease in wildlife vehicle collisions (WVC).

The map above utilizes data of salvaged carcesses from the Washington State Department of Transportation from 2020 to 2022. By clicking a data point the user can see the species found, date of collection, and county found. The collection of data is post construction of phase 1 and 2. Along the first 7 miles of the construction site there was one recorded salvaged carcass.

The wildlife crossing's effectiveness is still in question but the recent results have been promising. Along with limited carcasses found along I-90, the Conservation NW utilized game cameras to capture 4,286 wildlife crossings in 2020 and 4,440 in 2021.

Habitat Connectivity

The habitat connectivity map utilizes wildlife habitat connectivity data produced by the Washington Wildlife Habitat Connectivity Working Group (WHCWG). The map displays both the areas suitable for potential mule deer and the line features depicting linkages between core areas as straight lines. The map depicts there large polygons near the construction site of the I-90 project represented by the red line and bridge icon. The line features pass through this site connecting the north and south cascades for the wildlife population.
